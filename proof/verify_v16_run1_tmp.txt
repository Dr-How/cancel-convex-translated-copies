The solution is **not correct**. Here is a bug report.

1. **Location:**  
   “`T = {x_1,\dots,x_n} = supp(\sigma_T).`”  
   and  
   “`Assume for contradiction that T has \geq 3 edge-direction classes and both signs.`”

   **Description:** **Critical Error.**  
   The proof changes the problem. In the statement, \(n\) is the number of points of \(S\), the polygon is \(P=\operatorname{conv}(S)\), and \(T\) is an arbitrary finite signed translate set. The solution instead treats \(T\) itself as an \(n\)-vertex convex polygon and argues about its edge directions. That is a different claim from the one asked.

2. **Location:**  
   “`[u] = equivalence class of u under nonzero scalar multiples.`”

   **Description:** **Critical Error.**  
   This redefines the notion of direction class. The problem defines direction classes up to **positive** scalar multiples only; the solution uses **all nonzero** scalar multiples, which identifies opposite directions. This changes the hypothesis “at least three edge-direction classes” and affects later deductions.

3. **Location:**  
   “`KEY EQUATIONS:
   (NL) For p not in T:  sum_{j=1}^n sigma_T(p - s_j) = 0.
   (S0) For t in T, t != t_k:  sum_{j=1}^n sigma_T(t + s_k - s_j) = 0.`”  
   and  
   “`Closure: sum_{i=1}^n m_i * a_i = 0,  sum_{i=1}^n m_i = n.`”

   **Description:** **Justification Gap.**  
   These are taken as given but are not proved in the solution, and they are not among the two facts from Subproblems 1–4 that the problem explicitly allows without proof. The proof relies on them repeatedly, so the argument is incomplete.

4. **Location:**

5. **Location:**

But \(\beta(a_1)=0\) vanishes on the entire line spanned by \(a_1\), so \(\beta(a_3)\) could still be \(0\). The inference to \(\mu\neq0\) is invalid.
