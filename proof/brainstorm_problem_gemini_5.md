## Overall Insight

This is a problem in discrete geometry and additive combinatorics. The core objective is to analyze the cancellation patterns in the multiset sum $S+T$. The key insight is to combine the $L^2$-norm expansion of the sign function $\sigma(p)$ with the geometric properties of the Minkowski sum and convex polygons. 

The previous attempts failed because they relied on false assumptions about the number of translates ($|T|=2$) and unproven polynomial properties. To bypass these, we can leverage the exact algebraic identity $n(|T|-1) = \sum_{opp} m_{t_a-t_b} - \sum_{same} m_{t_a-t_b}$, where $m_v = |S \cap (S+v)|$. By definition, $m_v \leq 2$ for strictly convex polygons, and equality $m_v=2$ geometrically forces parallel edges (parallelograms) in $S$. Furthermore, we can rigorously prove that the support of $\sigma$ is exactly the set of extreme vertices of $\text{conv}(S+T)$ by considering directional maximizers. Together, these force heavily restricted geometric arrangements for $n \ge 5$ (too many parallelograms sharing diagonals) and $n=3$ (not enough parallel edges), inevitably collapsing the equality case to $n=4$ where $S$ is uniquely forced to be a parallelogram.

## Subproblem Decomposition

### Subproblem 1: Support Equivalency to Minkowski Vertices
**Statement**: Let $U = \{p \in \mathbb{R}^2 : \sigma(p) \neq 0\}$. First, use the Lonely Points Lemma to establish that $|U| \ge n$. Assume $|U| = n$. For any generic direction $w \in \mathbb{R}^2$, let $s \in S$ and $t \in T$ be the unique points maximizing the dot product with $w$. Prove that $s+t$ is the unique maximizer in $S+T$, hence $\sigma(s+t) = \sigma(t) \neq 0$. Conclude that $U$ contains all extreme vertices of $\text{conv}(S+T)$. Since $\text{conv}(S+T)$ has at least $|S|=n$ vertices, deduce that $U$ is exactly the set of vertices of $\text{conv}(S+T)$, and thus $\text{conv}(S+T)$ has exactly $n$ vertices.
**Role**: Establishes that all non-zero values of $\sigma$ lie exclusively on the strict geometric boundary of the Minkowski sum, and proves $|U| \ge n$ unconditionally.
**Approach**: Directional maximizers in a multiset sum are formed uniquely by the sum of individual maximizers. Uniqueness implies no cancellation occurs at these extreme vertices.
**Difficulty**: Medium

### Subproblem 2: The First Moment and L2 Norm Constraints
**Statement**: Let $k_1 = |T^+|$ and $k_2 = |T^-|$ where $k_1, k_2 \ge 1$. Prove that $||T^+| - |T^-|| \leq 1$. Then, expand the $L^2$-norm identity $\sum_{p \in \mathbb{R}^2} \sigma(p)^2 = n$ to prove $n(|T|-1) = \sum_{t_a \neq t_b} -\sigma(t_a)\sigma(t_b) |S \cap (S + t_a - t_b)|$. From this, letting $m_v = |S \cap (S+v)|$, derive the specific upper bound $n(k_1+k_2-1) \leq 4 k_1 k_2 - \sum_{\sigma(t_a)=\sigma(t_b)} m_{t_a - t_b}$.
**Role**: Sets up the governing algebraic inequalities linking the number of positive/negative translates to the geometric overlap counts $m_v$.
**Approach**: The first moment $\sum \sigma(p) = n(k_1-k_2)$ is bounded by the size of the support $n$ since $\sigma(p) \in \{-1, 1\}$. For the $L^2$ norm, expand $\sigma(p)^2$ via double sums, group terms by pairs $(t_a, t_b) \in T \times T$, and separate the same-sign and opposite-sign pairs, keeping in mind $m_v \le 2$.
**Difficulty**: Easy

### Subproblem 3: Overlap Equality and Shared Diagonals
**Statement**: Prove that for any strictly convex $n$-gon $S$, $m_v \le 2$ for all $v \neq 0$, and $m_v=2$ if and only if $v$ is a directed side of a parallelogram formed by four vertices of $S$. Furthermore, prove that if four distinct difference vectors $v_{13}, v_{24}, v_{14}, v_{23}$ generated by $t_1, t_2 \in T^+$ and $t_3, t_4 \in T^-$ all satisfy $m_v=2$ without reusing the same parallelogram side directions, the resulting parallelograms in $S$ must share a diagonal. Conclude that this requires $S$ to contain a centrally symmetric hexagon, forcing $n \geq 6$.
**Role**: Translates the maximal opposite-sign overlaps into an overly restrictive topological requirement on $S$, which will be used to rule out $n \ge 5$.
**Approach**: $m_v=2$ requires two parallel equal-length segments in $S$. The algebraic identity $(t_1-t_3) + (t_2-t_4) = (t_1-t_4) + (t_2-t_3)$ forces a linear relation $A+B = C+D$ between the sides of the parallelograms. Geometrically, this forces the parallelograms to share a diagonal, pulling 6 alternating vertices into a symmetric structure.
**Difficulty**: Hard

### Subproblem 4: Upper Bound on n
**Statement**: Assume $n \ge 5$. Use the bounds from Subproblem 2 and the geometric constraints from Subproblem 3 to derive a contradiction, thereby proving $n \leq 4$. Analyze the required number of $m_v=2$ overlaps for small $k_1, k_2$. If the $m_v=2$ vectors reuse the same parallelogram, show this forces equal differences in $T^+$ and $T^-$, which strictly increases the subtracted same-sign sum and drops the upper bound. If they use distinct parallelograms, invoke Subproblem 3 to show $|S| \geq 6$. Generalize this to eliminate all cases $n \geq 5$.
**Role**: Completes the proof of the upper bound $n \le 4$.
**Approach**: For example, $k_1=k_2=2 \implies 3n \le 16 \implies n \le 5$. To achieve $n=5$, we need at least seven of the eight opposite-sign pairs to be 2. If these come from a single parallelogram, same-sign differences are triggered, dropping the right-hand side. If from distinct parallelograms, Subproblem 3 applies and requires $n \ge 6$, which contradicts $n=5$.
**Difficulty**: Medium

### Subproblem 5: Ruling out n = 3
**Statement**: Prove that $n=3$ is impossible. Use the fact that for a triangle, $m_v \leq 1$ for all $v \neq 0$. Apply this to the $L^2$ bound to rule out all valid pairs of $(k_1, k_2)$ except potentially $k_1=3, k_2=2$. For this remaining case, prove that equality requires exactly $\{t^+ - t^- \mid t^+ \in T^+\}$ to be the three edges of $S$ for both $t^- \in T^-$. Show this forces $T^+$ to be a single translated copy of the edge set of $S$, which cannot hold simultaneously for two distinct points in $T^-$.
**Role**: Specifically eliminates the degenerate $n=3$ case where geometric bounds behave differently.
**Approach**: Triangles have no parallel edges, naturally dropping the $4 k_1 k_2$ theoretical bound to $2 k_1 k_2$. For $k_1=3, k_2=2$, substitute $m_v=1$ to realize it requires $T^+$ to surround each $t^-$ exactly as the edges of $S$, causing an origin-translation conflict.
**Difficulty**: Easy

### Subproblem 6: n = 4 forces a Parallelogram
**Statement**: Prove that if $n=4$ and the support has size exactly $4$, then $S$ must be the vertex set of a parallelogram.
**Role**: Proves the exact final structure of $S$ given the exact value of $n$.
**Approach**: For $n=4$, any valid combination of $k_1, k_2 \ge 1$ inputted into the bound $4(k_1+k_2-1) \leq 4 k_1 k_2 m_{v_{max}}$ strictly requires $m_v = 2$ for at least one vector $v \neq 0$. A strictly convex $4$-gon possessing a vector with overlap 2 must inherently be a parallelogram.
**Difficulty**: Easy

## Integration Sketch

By the Lonely Points Lemma, the support contains at least $n$ points. Subproblem 1 leverages directional extremes to show that if equality holds, the support uniquely isolates the vertices of the Minkowski sum $\text{conv}(S+T)$. Subproblem 2 formulates the $L^2$ expansion into a strict inequality connecting the first moment constraints with the geometric overlaps $m_v$. Subproblems 3 and 4 act as the geometric sieve: excessive maximal overlaps force either same-sign penalties or unachievable symmetric hexagonal structures, definitively capping $n \le 4$. Subproblem 5 rules out $n=3$ because a triangle inherently lacks the parallel edges needed to sustain the $L^2$ equilibrium. Finally, Subproblem 6 verifies the lone remaining possibility $n=4$, where the algebraic bound forces at least one $m_v=2$, inherently configuring the four vertices of $S$ into a parallelogram.
