## Overall Insight

This is a problem in discrete/convex geometry with an additive-combinatorial flavor: we are studying the signed convolution of the vertex set \(S\) of a convex polygon with a finite signed translate set \(T\), under the extremal assumption that exactly \(n=|S|\) points survive. By Subproblems 1–4, an equality case is already highly rigid on each boundary edge of \(Q=\operatorname{conv}(T)\): for every polygon edge direction \(a_i=s_{i+1}-s_i\), the exposed points of \(T\) on the corresponding side of \(Q\) form an arithmetic progression \(F_i\) with alternating signs.

The right strategy is to turn that local \(1\)-dimensional structure into a global obstruction. First, the alternating chains give a parity/monodromy rule when one walks once around \(\partial Q\). Second, any direction class that is to cause no extra support must be paired with a second chain in the same direction, with exactly the right length, phase, and placement. That kind of perfect pairing is compatible with a two-direction “checkerboard/parallelogram” geometry, but once three distinct edge-direction (parallelism) classes are present, the closed boundary walk of \(Q\) forces a detour through a third direction between two occurrences of one class. That detour makes the required pairing impossible, so some additional point of \(S+T\) survives with nonzero coefficient.

## Subproblem Decomposition

### Subproblem 1: Boundary walk and sign monodromy

**Statement**: In the setup where \(S=\{s_1,\dots,s_n\}\subset\mathbb R^2\) are the vertices of a convex \(n\)-gon \(P\) in cyclic order, \(T\subset\mathbb R^2\) is finite with sign map \(\sigma:T\to\{\pm1\}\), \(a_i=s_{i+1}-s_i\), and for each \(i\) one has
\[
F_i=T\cap[t_i,t_{i+1}]=\{t_i,t_i+a_i,\dots,t_i+m_i a_i\},
\]
with alternating signs, if \(\varepsilon_i:=\sigma(t_i)\) then \(\varepsilon_{i+1}=(-1)^{m_i}\varepsilon_i\) for every \(i\), and since \(t_{n+1}=t_1\) one gets
\[
\sum_{i=1}^n m_i a_i=0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\sum_{i=1}^n m_i\equiv 0\pmod 2.
\]

**Role**: This packages the local alternating-chain data into the two global constraints used later: geometric closure of the boundary walk of \(Q=\operatorname{conv}(T)\) and parity consistency of the sign assignment.

**Approach**: Traverse the progression \(F_i\) from \(t_i\) to \(t_{i+1}\); each step by \(a_i\) flips the sign once, so moving \(m_i\) steps multiplies the sign by \((-1)^{m_i}\). Summing the boundary increments gives the vector closure relation, and comparing the sign after one full circuit with the starting sign gives the even-parity condition.

**Difficulty**: easy

### Subproblem 2: Local cancellation on a single exposed chain

**Statement**: In the notation of Subproblem 1, let
\[
L:=\{s_i+t_i:1\le i\le n\}.
\]
Then for every \(i\) and every integer \(r\) with \(1\le r\le m_i\),
\[
s_i+t_i+r a_i=s_{i+1}+t_i+(r-1)a_i,
\]
and the two contributions at this point coming from the translates \(t_i+r a_i\) and \(t_i+(r-1)a_i\) have opposite signs; hence the entire exposed edge segment generated by \(F_i\) cancels except for its two endpoints in \(L\). Consequently, if
\[
\bigl|\{p\in\mathbb R^2:\sigma(p)\neq 0\}\bigr|=n,
\]
then any point \(p\in(S+T)\setminus L\) with \(\sigma(p)\neq 0\) must come from a failure of cancellation between distinct chains \(F_i\), not from the internal two-term cancellation on one chain.

**Role**: This isolates what is already harmless: each individual boundary progression cancels its own edge-interior contributions automatically. So any contradiction must come from how different direction classes interact globally.

**Approach**: Write down the two representations of the same point on the exposed edge of \(P+\operatorname{conv}(T)\) and use the alternating signs in \(F_i\) to see they sum to zero term-by-term.

**Difficulty**: easy

### Subproblem 3: Necessary matching for one edge-direction class

**Statement**: In the notation of Subproblem 1, fix one edge-direction (parallelism) class \(c\) of \(P\), and let \(L=\{s_r+t_r:1\le r\le n\}\). If
\[
\bigl|\{p\in\mathbb R^2:\sigma(p)\neq 0\}\bigr|=n,
\]
then \(c\) must be represented by exactly two edges of \(P\); after choosing a common oriented representative \(a\) of \(c\) and reindexing the corresponding progressions along that orientation,
\[
F_i=\{u_i+r a:0\le r\le m_i\},
\qquad
F_j=\{u_j+r a:0\le r\le m_j\},
\]
one must have
\[
m_i=m_j,\qquad \sigma(u_j)=-\sigma(u_i),
\qquad
s_i+u_i+\mathbb R a=s_j+u_j+\mathbb R a.
\]
If \(c\) occurs only once, or if any one of these matching conditions fails, then there exists a point \(p\in(S+T)\setminus L\) with \(\sigma(p)\neq 0\).

**Role**: This is the key “pairing lemma” for a single direction class: an extremal configuration can only survive if every class is matched by a second, perfectly aligned chain of the same length and opposite phase.

**Approach**: Use a supporting functional orthogonal to \(a\) to isolate the contributions from this class. Compare the first/last unmatched point along the common direction: a missing partner, unequal chain lengths, wrong phase, or different carrier line leaves a visible uncanceled point outside \(L\).

**Difficulty**: hard

### Subproblem 4: A third direction breaks the pairings

**Statement**: In the notation of Subproblem 1, assume \(P\) has at least three distinct edge-direction classes and that every class satisfies the matching conclusion of Subproblem 3. Choose the first pair \(i<j\) in cyclic order with \([a_i]=[a_j]\) and some index \(k\) satisfying \(i<k<j\) and \([a_k]\neq [a_i]\). Then
\[
t_j-t_i=\sum_{r=i}^{j-1} m_r a_r
\]
has a nonzero component in a third direction, so the second chain cannot lie on the unique carrier line required to match the first chain without either identifying two distinct elements of \(T\) or creating a point \(p\in(S+T)\setminus L\) with \(\sigma(p)\neq 0\), where \(L=\{s_r+t_r:1\le r\le n\}\).

**Role**: This is the genuine global obstruction. It shows that even if each class is locally forced to pair perfectly, those pairings cannot all coexist around the closed boundary once a third direction class appears.

**Approach**: Follow the endpoints using \(t_{r+1}=t_r+m_r a_r\). For the first repeated class, the intervening detour passes through a genuinely different direction, so the displacement between the two candidate matching chains is not parallel to the class itself. Compare this with the line-coincidence demanded by Subproblem 3 to force either overlap/repetition in \(T\) or an extra surviving point in \(S+T\).

**Difficulty**: hard

### Subproblem 5: Final exclusion of the three-direction case

**Statement**: Deduce from Subproblems 1–4 that if \(P=\operatorname{conv}(S)\) has at least three distinct edge-direction classes, then no finite signed set \(T\subset\mathbb R^2\) with both signs present can satisfy
\[
\bigl|\{p\in\mathbb R^2:\sigma(p)\neq 0\}\bigr|=n.
\]
Hence every triangle, every convex \(n\)-gon with \(n\ge 5\), and every non-parallelogram convex quadrilateral are excluded, leaving only the case \(n=4\) with exactly two edge-direction classes.

**Role**: This assembles the contradiction and extracts the promised corollaries.

**Approach**: Combine the local pairing requirement from Subproblem 3 with the global incompatibility from Subproblem 4. If a class is unpaired, Subproblem 3 already gives an extra nonzero point; if all classes are paired, Subproblem 4 rules out three or more classes.

**Difficulty**: easy

## Integration Sketch

Assuming the subproblems are solved, the proof runs as follows. Subproblem 1 turns the alternating arithmetic progressions on the sides of \(Q=\operatorname{conv}(T)\) into a closed boundary walk with a parity constraint on signs. Subproblem 2 shows that each single chain already cancels its own exposed edge-interior contributions, so any contradiction must come from interactions between different chains. Subproblem 3 then says that, in an equality case, each edge-direction class of \(P\) would have to occur as a perfectly matched pair of parallel chains: same length, opposite phase, same carrier line. If some class is unpaired or mismatched, an extra point of \(S+T\) with nonzero coefficient appears immediately. Finally, Subproblem 4 proves that such perfect pairings cannot be realized globally when at least three direction classes are present, because the closed walk in \(Q\) necessarily inserts a detour through a third direction between two occurrences of one class, breaking the required alignment. Therefore the extremal equality is impossible for all polygons with at least three edge-direction classes, so the only remaining equality candidate is a quadrilateral with exactly two classes, i.e. a parallelogram.
