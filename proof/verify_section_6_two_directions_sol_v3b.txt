### 1. Omitted case in the parallel-edge analysis
**Location:**  
> “If \(e_i\) and \(e_{i+1}\) are parallel (meaning \(e_{i+1}=\lambda e_i\) for some \(\lambda\neq 0\)), then … If \(\lambda>0\) … If \(-1<\lambda<0\) … If \(\lambda<-1\) … In each case one of the three vertices lies strictly between the other two … Hence no two adjacent edges are parallel.”

**Description:** **Justification Gap**  
The case \(\lambda=-1\) is not handled. For \(\lambda=-1\), one gets
\[
e_{i+1}=-e_i \quad\Rightarrow\quad v_{i+2}-v_i=e_i+e_{i+1}=0,
\]
so \(v_{i+2}=v_i\). The proof’s case split does not cover this possibility before concluding “Hence no two adjacent edges are parallel.” So the argument is incomplete as written.

### 2. Undefined/ambiguous use of \(\arg\)
**Location:**  
> “Let \(\theta_1:=\arg(e_1)\) be the argument of \(e_1\), and define recursively  
> \[
> \theta_{i+1}:=\theta_i+\varepsilon_i.
> \]
> We claim that \(\theta_i=\arg(e_i)\) for all \(i\): … since \(\varepsilon_i\) is the counterclockwise turning angle from \(e_i\) to \(e_{i+1}\), we have \(\arg(e_{i+1})=\arg(e_i)+\varepsilon_i\).”

**Description:** **Justification Gap**  
The proof uses \(\arg\) as though it were a single-valued real function satisfying
\[
\arg(e_{i+1})=\arg(e_i)+\varepsilon_i,
\]
but no branch (or lifted/unwrapped angle) is specified. Arguments are only defined modulo \(2\pi\) unless a specific convention is fixed. Without explicitly choosing a consistent real-valued branch/lift, the induction claim \(\theta_i=\arg(e_i)\), and hence the later conclusion that the directed edge directions are all distinct within one full turn, is not rigorously justified.
