## Overall Insight

This problem belongs to the intersection of combinatorial geometry and additive combinatorics. It asks us to characterize when a signed Minkowski sum of a convex point set $S$ and a translation set $T$ achieves its minimal possible support size.

The key insight is to translate the cancellation condition into an algebraic relation using Laurent polynomials. Let $S(x,y)$ and $T(x,y)$ be the indicator polynomials of the sets, so that their product $\Sigma(x,y) = S(x,y)T(x,y)$ represents the signed sum. By multiplying this equation by the dual polynomials (e.g., $S(x^{-1},y^{-1})$) and extracting the constant term, we obtain an exact $L_2$ norm equation: $n = n|T| + \sum_{z \neq 0} C_S(z) \tilde{C}_T(z)$, where $C_S(z)$ is the number of times the vector $z$ appears as a difference between points in $S$. 

This approach is highly effective because it cleanly separates the geometric structure of $S$ from the sign distribution of $T$. It bypasses the combinatorial nightmare of manually tracking individual point cancellations. Because $S$ is in strictly convex position, any non-zero vector can occur as a difference at most twice ($C_S(z) \leq 2$). This geometrically bounds the maximum possible negative cancellation in the algebraic equation, directly forcing $n \leq 4$.

## Subproblem Decomposition

### Subproblem 1: Lower Bound via Lonely Points

**Statement**: Let $S \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ be a finite set of $n \geq 1$ points in strictly convex position, and $T \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ a finite set of translating vectors with assigned signs $\sigma(t) \in \{+1, -1\}$. For $p \in \mathbb{R}^2$, define $\sigma(p) = \sum_{t \in T: p-t \in S} \sigma(t)$. Prove that $|\{p \in \mathbb{R}^2 : \sigma(p) \neq 0\}| \geq n$.

**Role**: Establishes the minimal baseline size of the support. This implies that when $|\text{supp}(\sigma)| = n$, the non-zero points are exactly the $n$ strictly extremal points of $S+T$, meaning all non-extremal generated points must perfectly cancel.

**Approach**: Apply the Lonely Points Lemma. For each $s \in S$, pick a generic direction $v$ in its normal cone such that $s$ uniquely maximizes the inner product with $v$ over $S$. Let $t_v \in T$ maximize the inner product with $v$ over $T$. Show that $s+t_v$ is strictly extremal in $S+T$ in direction $v$, making it uniquely formed (a "lonely point"), thus giving it a non-zero coefficient in the sum.

**Difficulty**: Easy

### Subproblem 2: Bounding the Support Size to $n \leq 4$

**Statement**: Let $S \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ be a set of $n \geq 3$ points in strictly convex position, and let $T \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ be a finite set of translating vectors ($|T| \geq 2$) containing at least one $+1$ sign and at least one $-1$ sign. If $|\{p \in \mathbb{R}^2 : \sigma(p) \neq 0\}| = n$, prove that $n \leq 4$.

**Role**: Drastically restricts the possible sizes of $S$ that can achieve the equality condition, isolating $n=3$ and $n=4$ as the only potential candidates.

**Approach**: Use the polynomial formulation $S(x,y)T(x,y) = \Sigma(x,y)$. Multiply by $S(x^{-1},y^{-1})T(x^{-1},y^{-1})$ and evaluate the constant term to get $n = n|T| + \sum_{z \neq 0} C_S(z) \tilde{C}_T(z)$, where $C_S$ and $\tilde{C}_T$ are the difference count functions of $S$ and the signed difference count of $T$. Use the geometric fact that a strictly convex polygon has $C_S(z) \leq 2$ to bound the negative cross-terms generated by opposite signs in $T$. This limits the negative sum to at most $-4|T^+||T^-|$, which algebraically forces $n(|T|-1) \leq 4|T^+||T^-|$ and limits $n \leq 4$.

**Difficulty**: Hard

### Subproblem 3: Ruling Out the Triangle Case

**Statement**: Under the identical hypotheses of Subproblem 2, prove that $n \neq 3$.

**Role**: Eliminates $n=3$ from the remaining possibilities, establishing that any configuration meeting the equality condition must have exactly $n=4$ points.

**Approach**: For $n=3$, $S$ is a triangle. No two edges of a triangle are parallel, meaning $C_S(z) \leq 1$ for all non-zero $z$. Furthermore, $S$ has exactly $n(n-1) = 6$ non-zero differences. Substitute $C_S(z) \leq 1$ and the support size of 6 into the difference polynomial equation $n(1 - |T|) = \sum_{z \neq 0} C_S(z) \tilde{C}_T(z)$. Show that the limited number of available differences cannot provide a sufficiently negative sum to satisfy the algebraic balance for any valid sizes of $T^+$ and $T^-$.

**Difficulty**: Medium

### Subproblem 4: Characterization of the Quadrilateral Case

**Statement**: Under the identical hypotheses of Subproblem 2, if $n = 4$, prove that the $4$ vertices of $S$ must form a parallelogram.

**Role**: Completes the proof of the conjecture by identifying the precise geometric structure of $S$ for the only remaining valid case ($n=4$).

**Approach**: For $n=4$, satisfying the difference polynomial equation requires maximizing the negative cancellations. This forces the upper bound $C_S(z) \leq 2$ to be achieved with equality for the vectors $z$ that match the opposite-signed pairs in $T$. Because $S$ must contain non-zero vectors $z$ that appear twice as differences between its vertices, $S$ must possess parallel opposite chords of the same length. A convex quadrilateral with parallel opposite edges of equal length is a parallelogram.

**Difficulty**: Medium

## Integration Sketch

To prove the main conjecture, we compose the subproblems sequentially. First, Subproblem 1 guarantees that the support of the signed sum is at least $n$, ensuring that the equality case $|\text{supp}(\sigma)|=n$ implies all non-extremal points are completely cancelled. Utilizing this cancellation structurally, Subproblem 2 employs difference polynomials to restrict the maximum number of points to $n \leq 4$ by bounding the capacity of a strictly convex polygon to generate canceling pairs. Subproblem 3 filters out $n=3$ by observing that a triangle lacks the parallel chords necessary to meet the required cancellation quota. Finally, Subproblem 4 concludes that for the surviving case $n=4$ to algebraically satisfy the cancellation constraints, it must possess pairs of parallel chords, forcing the convex quadrilateral $S$ to be a parallelogram.
