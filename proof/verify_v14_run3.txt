[Run1,Run2,Run3] CE-01: Wrong object studied throughout.
  Location: Opening setup -- "We have x_1,...,x_n in convex position (CCW),
  sigma_T finitely supported on T = {x_1,...,x_n}."
  The problem has S as the convex n-gon and T as the signed translate set.
  The proof identifies T with the convex polygon and with the support of sigma.
  Every subsequent deduction is about the wrong object.

[Run2,Run3] CE-02: Direction class definition silently widened.
  Location: "The direction class [a_i] is the equivalence class under nonzero
  scalar multiples."
  The problem uses positive scalar multiples. Enlarging to all nonzero multiples
  is not proved equivalent and changes the count.

[Run1,Run2,Run3] CE-03: (S0) equation for n=3 has wrong (extra) term count.
  Location: n=3 lemma, equation applied with k=2:
    sigma_T(t'+a_1)+sigma_T(t')+sigma_T(t'-a_2)+sigma_T(t'-a_2-a_3)=0.
  For n=3, (S0) has only THREE summands. The proof inserts a fourth, and since
  t'-a_2-a_3=t'+a_1 the claimed coefficient-2 relation is doubly invalid.
  This error appears at least twice in the n=3 section.

[Run1,Run2,Run3] CE-04: n=3 case split abandoned; existence of opposite-sign
  point at positive height not established.
  Location: "This is getting complicated. Let me use the cleaner approach: for
  n=3, ANY t' in T with h(t')>0 works."
  The h(t')=0 sub-case is never resolved. The proof asserts but does not prove
  that an opposite-sign point with h(t')>0 exists. Case split is incomplete.

[Run1,Run2,Run3] CE-05: Lemma hzero (n=4): invalid vanishing of fourth term
  when mu<0.
  Location: "For n=4: sigma_T(q+a_1)+sigma_T(q)+0+0=0 (heights 0,0,-1,-1-mu)."
  If mu<0, then -1-mu can be 0 or positive; the fourth term cannot be discarded.
  The two-term relation that drives subsequent contradiction is derived from a
  truncated equation.

[Run1,Run2,Run3] CE-06: Lemma cross -- cross-point computed at wrong location.
  Location: "apply (NL) at p = s_1+(t_2+a_2) = t_1+a_2."
  Since t_2=t_1+m_1*a_1, we have s_1+(t_2+a_2)=t_1+m_1*a_1+a_2 for m_1>=1,
  not t_1+a_2. The cross-point equation is evaluated at the wrong point.

[Run1,Run2,Run3] CE-07: (NL) applied without establishing p lies outside support.
  Location: Lemma cross -- "possible but a degenerate subcase; in the non-
  degenerate case p not in T."
  The proof explicitly admits p may lie in the support T. The hypothesis of (NL)
  is never satisfied; degenerate cases are silently dropped without analysis.

[Run2] CE-08: Same paragraph identifies t_1+a_2 = t_2+a_2 when m_1>=1.
  Location: Lemma cross -- "sigma_T(t_1+a_2) = sigma_T(t_2+a_2)."
  The same paragraph notes t_1+a_2 != t_2+a_2 when m_1>=1. The identification
  is contradicted by the proof's own statement.

[Run1,Run2,Run3] CE-09: Lemmas cross and cross1 stated for all mu, proved only
  for mu>0, then cited for all non-integer mu.
  Location: "So Lemma cross1 is proved for mu>0. (The case mu<0 is handled in
  Prop n4muZ.)" -- but Prop n4muZ handles only integer mu, leaving negative
  non-integer mu entirely uncovered. Prop n4mu then cites these lemmas as if
  fully proved.

[Run2,Run3] CE-10: False arithmetic claim in Prop n4mu (m_1=0, m_2=0 sub-case).
  Location: "If mu is rational non-integer: mu=p/q with q>=2; then
  mu=1/(k_0-1-j) requires q=1: contradiction."
  Counterexample: mu=1/2 is rational non-integer and equals 1/(k_0-1-j) for
  k_0-1-j=2. The supposed contradiction does not exist.

[Run3] CE-11: Prop n4muZ closure -- false geometric inference.
  Location: "From beta(a_3)=-ell the proof concludes a_3 is a scalar multiple
  of a_1."
  A linear functional taking the same value on two vectors does not imply they
  are parallel.

[Run1,Run2,Run3] CE-12: Lemma hzero-n (n>=5): c_j>0 does not give -c_j<=-1.
  Location: "extra j>=5 terms have heights -c_j<=-1<0 (for mu<0: c_j>0 by

  c_j>0 only gives -c_j<0, not -c_j<=-1. The vanishing of extra terms in the

[Run1,Run2,Run3] CE-13: (NL) applied at point whose non-membership in support
  is only "likely" or "in general."
  Location: Lemma cross1 -- "for generic mu these are not in T" / "likely not

[Run1,Run2,Run3] CE-14: Final conclusion addresses the wrong object.
  Location: "Therefore, T can have at most 2 edge-direction classes."
